# California Regional Centers: Services for Children with Developmental Disabilities

**What are Regional Centers:** 21 community-based nonprofit organizations that provide services and supports for Californians with developmental disabilities from birth through adulthood.

**Key law:** Lanterman Developmental Disabilities Act (1977) — guarantees the right to services enabling independent, integrated community living.

**Overseen by:** California Department of Developmental Services (DDS)

*Regional Center Directory (21 centers): Verified 2025-12-23 against dds.ca.gov/rc/listings/*

---

## What Regional Centers Do

Regional Centers are your primary gateway for:

1. **Assessment & Diagnosis** — Determine if your child has a qualifying developmental disability
2. **Service Coordination** — Assign a case manager to help plan and access services
3. **Service Purchase** — Fund specialized services included in your child's plan
4. **Advocacy** — Protect your child's legal, civil, and service rights
5. **Community Connection** — Help find resources, providers, and support

## Is My Child Eligible?

### Qualifying Developmental Disabilities

To qualify, your child must have one of these conditions that:
- Began **before age 18**
- Is expected to **continue indefinitely**
- Results in **substantial disability** (significant limitations in 3+ areas of daily life)

| Qualifying Conditions |
|----------------------|
| Intellectual disability |
| Cerebral palsy |
| Epilepsy |
| Autism spectrum disorder |
| Other conditions requiring similar treatment |

**NOT qualifying:** Physical disabilities alone, learning disabilities alone, or mental health conditions alone.

### Substantial Disability = Limitations in 3+ Life Areas

| Life Area | Examples |
|-----------|----------|
| **Self-care** | Eating, dressing, hygiene |
| **Language** | Understanding and expressing communication |
| **Learning** | Acquiring new skills, academic abilities |
| **Mobility** | Walking, moving, using transportation |
| **Self-direction** | Making decisions, managing daily activities |
| **Independent living** | Cooking, cleaning, managing a household |
| **Economic self-sufficiency** | Working, managing money |

### Special Rules for Young Children

**Infants and Toddlers (0-3 years) — Early Start Program:**
- Broader eligibility — don't need full diagnosis
- Qualify if they have developmental delay OR established risk condition OR high biomedical risk
- Serves children "at risk" of developmental disabilities

**Children Ages 0-4 — Provisional Eligibility:**
- Qualify with limitations in only **2** life areas (instead of 3)
- Allows access to services while formal diagnosis is still developing

## Services Relevant to Child Care

### Respite Care

**What it is:** Temporary relief for family caregivers — someone else cares for your child so you can take a break.

| Respite Type | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| **In-home respite** | Caregiver comes to your home |
| **Out-of-home respite** | Your child goes to a licensed facility |
| **Behavioral respite** | Trained staff who can handle behavioral challenges |
| **Crisis respite** | 24-hour emergency care during acute situations |

**Why it matters for childcare:** Respite allows parents to work, attend school, manage other family needs, or simply rest from the demands of caring for a child with significant needs.

### Early Intervention Services (Early Start)

For infants and toddlers, Early Start provides:
- Speech and language therapy
- Occupational and physical therapy
- Special instruction
- Family training and counseling
- Developmental assessments
- Service coordination

### Day Programs

Community-based programs that help children develop:
- Self-help and self-care skills
- Social interaction abilities
- Communication skills
- Behavior management
- Community participation skills

### Behavioral Services

- Assessment of challenging behaviors
- Development of behavior support plans
- Training for family members
- Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) for autism
- Crisis intervention

### Coordination with Child Care Subsidies

Regional Centers can help:
- Identify inclusive childcare providers
- Coordinate with APPs (Alternative Payment Programs)
- Access enhanced reimbursement rates for special needs care
- Support providers in making accommodations

## The Application Process

### Step 1: Contact Your Regional Center

Find your Regional Center based on where you live (see directory below) and call to request intake.

### Step 2: Initial Intake (Within 15 working days)

The Regional Center will:
- Meet with you and gather basic information
- Collect releases to obtain records from doctors, schools, etc.
- Decide whether to proceed with full assessment

### Step 3: Assessment (Within 120 days)

- Comprehensive evaluation by clinical team
- Review of medical, psychological, educational records
- Developmental testing
- Social assessment of family background

**Urgent cases:** If delay would risk your child's health, safety, or development — assessment within **60 days**.

### Step 4: Eligibility Determination

After assessment, the Regional Center determines:
- Does your child have a qualifying developmental disability?
- For children 0-4: Do they qualify for provisional eligibility?
- For infants/toddlers: Do they qualify for Early Start?

### Step 5: Individual Program Plan (IPP)

If eligible, within **60 days** you'll develop an IPP with your service coordinator:
- Identify your child's goals and needs
- Determine which services will help
- Document the plan in writing
- Sign the agreement

## What You'll Pay

### Most Services Are FREE

Regional Center services are generally provided **at no cost** regardless of family income. This includes:
- Eligibility assessment and diagnosis
- Service coordination (case management)
- Advocacy and information
- Most Early Start services
- Most respite, therapy, and behavioral services

### When Fees Apply

| Situation | Fee |
|-----------|-----|
| **24-hour out-of-home placement** (child under 18) | Parental cost-sharing based on ability to pay |
| **Respite, day care, camping** (if income ≥ 4x poverty level AND child not on Medi-Cal) | 5-80% of cost on sliding scale |
| **Annual Family Program Fee** (if income ≥ 4x poverty level) | $150-$200/year |

### Who Pays NO Fees

- Families where child qualifies for Medi-Cal
- Families with income below 4x federal poverty level
- Families experiencing catastrophic loss (exemption available)

## Your Rights Under the Lanterman Act

The Lanterman Act guarantees people with developmental disabilities:

- Treatment in natural community settings
- Services in the least restrictive environment
- Dignity, privacy, and humane care
- Freedom from harm and unnecessary restraints
- Participation in appropriate education
- Choice in where to live and how to spend time
- The right to make their own life decisions

**Appeal rights:** If you disagree with a Regional Center decision, you can request a fair hearing within 60 days.

## How Regional Centers Work with Child Care

### Finding Inclusive Child Care

Regional Centers can help you:
- Identify providers who serve children with disabilities
- Understand what accommodations your child needs
- Connect with childcare subsidy programs (CalWORKs, CCDF)
- Access enhanced reimbursement rates for specialized care

### Coordinating Services

Your service coordinator can:
- Attend childcare planning meetings
- Help write behavioral support plans for childcare settings
- Connect childcare providers with training resources
- Ensure consistency across home, childcare, and therapy

### Specialized Programs

- **Severely Handicapped (CHAN) Program** — Dedicated childcare slots for children with significant disabilities
- **Inclusive Early Education Expansion Program (IEEEP)** — Grants for providers to become more inclusive
- **Enhanced rates** — Higher reimbursement for providers serving children with special needs
