# Public Hearing Participation

## Types of Hearings

### Adjudicative Hearings (Evidentiary)

Quasi-judicial proceedings for determination of facts, resulting in binding decisions affecting specific parties.

**Examples:**
- Waste discharge requirements hearings
- NPDES permit hearings
- Enforcement orders
- Water rights application and petition hearings

**Characteristics:**
- Evidence received under oath
- Findings of fact and conclusions of law
- Directly affects legal rights of parties
- Due process protections required

### Informational Hearings

Designed to gather facts, opinions, and information to assist the Boards in policy formulation.

**Examples:**
- Policy development workshops
- Basin plan amendment hearings
- Rulemaking proceedings
- Informational sessions

**Characteristics:**
- No binding decisions on specific parties
- Broad input gathering
- Less formal procedures
- Equal opportunity for all participants

## Requesting to Speak

### At Board Meetings

1. Submit a speaker request card before the agenda item
2. Indicate which item you wish to address
3. Be prepared to speak when called

### Time Limits

| Speaker Type | Typical Time |
|-------------|--------------|
| Interested parties (general) | 3 minutes |
| Designated parties | Up to 10 minutes |

**Note**: The Board Chair may adjust time limits based on the number of speakers and complexity of the matter.

## Oral Testimony Procedures

### Informational Items

- Comments should clarify or add to the Board's understanding
- Comments should NOT be testimonial or argumentative
- Speakers are not under oath
- Proceeding is not adversarial

### Adjudicative Proceedings

- Testimony is given under oath
- Witnesses may be cross-examined
- Evidence rules apply (Gov. Code Section 11513)
- Formal record is created

### General Rules

- Written comments shall NOT be read into the record (unless allowed by Chair)
- Focus oral testimony on new information or clarifications
- Honor time limits to allow others to speak

## Designated Parties vs. Interested Persons

### Designated Parties

In adjudicative proceedings, designated parties include:

- Board staff
- Discharger or responsible party
- Persons directly affected by the discharge
- Others designated by the Board

**Rights of designated parties:**
- Present evidence
- Call witnesses
- Cross-examine other parties' witnesses
- Present rebuttal testimony
- Deliver closing arguments

### Interested Persons

Persons who are not designated parties:

- May provide input during designated portions
- Speak after designated parties present their cases
- Do NOT have cross-examination rights
- May ask the Board to clarify testimony (at Board's discretion)

### Requesting Party Status

To become a designated party:

1. Submit written request before deadline in hearing notice
2. Explain basis for party status
3. Describe how you are directly affected by the matter
4. File request with the Board

## Ex Parte Communication Rules

### What is Ex Parte Communication?

Communication to a board member about a pending adjudicative matter that:

- Occurs without other parties present
- Occurs without notice to other parties
- Denies other parties opportunity to participate

### When Does the Prohibition Apply?

- Once an adjudicative proceeding is pending or impending
- Typically triggered by the hearing notice
- Continues until final decision is made

### What is Prohibited?

For adjudicative proceedings:

- One-sided communications with board members
- Discussions about substantive issues in the proceeding
- Communications even if technically "public" but without notice to parties

### What is NOT Prohibited?

- Rulemaking and legislative proceedings (ex parte may be allowed)
- Communications with staff (not decision-makers) about factual matters
- Written submissions to the official record through proper procedures
- Communications not directed at board members

### Consequences of Violation

- May invalidate the Board's decision
- Creates grounds for legal challenge
- Undermines public trust in the process

### Uncertainty?

If unsure whether a communication would violate ex parte rules:

- Contact the Office of Chief Counsel
- Staff can provide guidance on permissible communications

## Order of Proceedings

### Adjudicative Hearings

1. Opening statement by chairperson/presiding officer
2. Identification of participants
3. Administration of oath to witnesses
4. Presentation of exhibits by staff
5. Presentation of evidence by parties
6. Cross-examination of witnesses
7. Redirect and recross-examination
8. Questions from Board members
9. Procedural motions

### Informational/Rulemaking Proceedings

1. Opening statement by Board
2. Presentation by Board staff
3. Presentations by all other persons
4. Recommendation by Executive Officer (if appropriate)
5. Close of hearing
6. Board deliberation and voting

## Rules of Evidence

### Adjudicative Hearings

- Government Code Section 11513 applies
- Less restrictive than civil courts
- Hearsay evidence is generally admissible
- Flexibility to present fuller picture of facts
- Parties may respond to adverse evidence

### Weight of Evidence

The Board determines:

- What weight to give evidence
- Credibility of witnesses
- Sufficiency of proof

## Preparing for a Hearing

### Before the Hearing

1. Review the hearing notice carefully
2. Understand deadlines for party status requests
3. Identify witnesses you may want to call
4. Prepare exhibits and evidence
5. Submit materials by required deadlines

### During the Hearing

1. Arrive early and check in
2. Follow directions from presiding officer
3. Be prepared when called
4. Speak clearly and stay within time limits
5. Be respectful to Board members and other parties

### After the Hearing

1. Review response-to-comments documents
2. Monitor for final decision
3. Understand appeal rights if applicable
