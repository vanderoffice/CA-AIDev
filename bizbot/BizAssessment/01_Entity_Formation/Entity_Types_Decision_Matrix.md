## Detailed Entity Breakdown

### Sole Proprietorship

**What it is:** Not a separate legal entity - you ARE the business.

**Formation:**
- No state filing required
- May need DBA/Fictitious Business Name (county filing, $10-80)
- Get EIN from IRS if hiring or want to keep SSN private

**Taxes:**
- Report on personal return (Schedule C)
- Pay self-employment tax (15.3% on net profit)
- Quarterly estimated taxes required

**Pros:**
- Simplest and cheapest to start
- Complete control
- No annual fees or reports
- Pass-through taxation

**Cons:**
- Unlimited personal liability
- Hard to raise capital
- Business ends when owner dies
- Less credible for some clients/partners

**Best for:** Freelancers, consultants, side businesses, testing a business idea

---

### Limited Liability Company (LLC)

**What it is:** Hybrid entity with liability protection of a corporation and tax flexibility of a partnership.

**Formation:**
- File Articles of Organization with SOS ($70)
- Create Operating Agreement (not filed, but essential)
- File Statement of Information within 90 days ($20)

**Taxes:**
- Default: Pass-through (single-member = Schedule C, multi-member = Form 1065)
- Can elect S-Corp or C-Corp taxation
- $800 minimum franchise tax (waived first year for certain new LLCs)
- LLC fee if CA gross receipts exceed $250,000

| CA Gross Receipts | Annual LLC Fee |
|-------------------|---------------|
| $250,000 - $499,999 | $900 |
| $500,000 - $999,999 | $2,500 |
| $1,000,000 - $4,999,999 | $6,000 |
| $5,000,000+ | $11,790 |

**Pros:**
- Personal liability protection
- Flexible profit distribution
- Less formality than corporations
- Tax flexibility (choose your taxation)
- Can have unlimited members

**Cons:**
- $800 annual minimum tax
- Self-employment tax on all profit (unless S-Corp election)
- Potential LLC fee for high-revenue businesses
- Some states don't recognize LLCs fully

**Best for:** Most small businesses, real estate investors, multi-member businesses

---

### S-Corporation

**What it is:** Corporation that elects pass-through taxation under IRS Subchapter S.

**Formation:**
- File Articles of Incorporation with SOS ($100)
- Adopt bylaws, issue stock, hold initial meeting
- File IRS Form 2553 to elect S-Corp status
- File Statement of Information annually ($25)

**Taxes:**
- Pass-through to shareholders (Form 1120S + Schedule K-1)
- Shareholders who work in business must take "reasonable salary"
- Only wages subject to payroll tax (not distributions)
- $800 minimum franchise tax
- 1.5% tax on net income (minimum $800)

**Self-Employment Tax Savings Example:**
| Scenario | LLC | S-Corp |
|----------|-----|--------|
| Net profit | $150,000 | $150,000 |
| Reasonable salary | N/A | $80,000 |
| SE/Payroll tax base | $150,000 | $80,000 |
| SE/Payroll tax (15.3%) | $22,950 | $12,240 |
| **Tax savings** | — | **$10,710** |

**Restrictions:**
- Max 100 shareholders
- Only one class of stock
- Shareholders must be US citizens/residents
- No corporate, partnership, or non-resident alien shareholders

**Pros:**
- Avoid self-employment tax on distributions
- Pass-through taxation
- Established corporate structure
- Credibility with clients/vendors

**Cons:**
- Must pay reasonable salary (payroll complexity)
- More formalities than LLC
- Restrictions on ownership
- $800 annual minimum tax

**Best for:** Profitable businesses ($70K+ net income), owners who want to minimize self-employment tax

---

### C-Corporation

**What it is:** Standard corporation - separate legal entity from its owners.

**Formation:**
- File Articles of Incorporation with SOS ($100)
- Adopt bylaws, issue stock, elect directors, appoint officers
- File Statement of Information annually ($25)

**Taxes:**
- Separate corporate tax return (Form 1120)
- Federal corporate tax rate: 21% flat
- CA corporate tax rate: 8.84%
- Dividends taxed again at shareholder level ("double taxation")
- $800 minimum franchise tax

**Pros:**
- Best for raising investment (VCs, angels)
- Unlimited shareholders, multiple stock classes
- No restrictions on ownership
- Can retain earnings at corporate level
- Stock options and equity compensation flexibility
- Perpetual existence

**Cons:**
- Double taxation on dividends
- Most complex and expensive to maintain
- Extensive recordkeeping requirements
- $800 annual minimum tax

**Best for:** Businesses seeking investment, planning to go public, high-growth startups

---

### General Partnership (GP)

**What it is:** Two or more people doing business together without forming an entity.

**Formation:**
- No state filing required
- Should have written Partnership Agreement
- May need DBA filing

**Taxes:**
- Partnership return (Form 1065)
- Income passes through to partners (Schedule K-1)
- Partners pay self-employment tax

**Pros:**
- Simple to form
- No annual state fees
- Flexible profit sharing
- Pass-through taxation

**Cons:**
- All partners have unlimited personal liability
- Each partner can bind the partnership
- Less formal structure can cause disputes
- Business dissolves if partner leaves/dies

**Best for:** Informal joint ventures, professional practices (with caution)

---

### Limited Partnership (LP) / Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

**LP - What it is:** Partnership with general partners (manage, unlimited liability) and limited partners (invest only, limited liability).

**LLP - What it is:** Partnership where all partners have limited liability for the partnership's debts and each other's actions.

**Formation:**
- LP: File Certificate of Limited Partnership with SOS ($70)
- LLP: File Registration with SOS ($70)
- Both: File Statement of Information biennially ($20)

**Taxes:**
- Partnership return (Form 1065)
- $800 minimum franchise tax
- Pass-through to partners

**LLP Restrictions:**
- Only available to licensed professionals (lawyers, accountants, architects, engineers)

**Pros:**
- Limited liability for limited partners (LP) or all partners (LLP)
- Good for passive investors (LP)
- Protects professionals from partners' malpractice (LLP)
- Pass-through taxation

**Cons:**
- $800 annual minimum tax
- GP in LP has unlimited liability
- LLP limited to certain professions
- More complex than GP

**Best for:** Real estate syndications (LP), professional firms (LLP)

## Decision Flowchart

```
START: Are you the only owner?
│
├─ YES → Do you need liability protection?
│         │
│         ├─ NO → Sole Proprietorship
│         │
│         └─ YES → Will you earn >$70K net profit?
│                   │
│                   ├─ NO → Single-Member LLC
│                   │
│                   └─ YES → LLC with S-Corp Election
│
└─ NO → Are you seeking outside investment?
         │
         ├─ YES → C-Corporation
         │
         └─ NO → Do all owners work in the business?
                  │
                  ├─ YES → Multi-Member LLC (consider S-Corp election)
                  │
                  └─ NO → Limited Partnership or LLC
```

## California-Specific Considerations

### The $800 Minimum Tax Reality

**Important:** LLCs, LPs, LLPs, and Corporations pay $800/year minimum, even if they lose money.

**First-Year Exemption (limited):**
- New LLCs formed after 1/1/2021 may be exempt from the $800 minimum tax in their first taxable year
- Does NOT apply to corporations
- Must not be a successor business

### Statement of Information Requirements

| Entity | Frequency | Fee | Deadline |
|--------|-----------|-----|----------|
| LLC | Biennial | $20 | Every 2 years from formation |
| LP/LLP | Biennial | $20 | Every 2 years from formation |
| Corporation | Annual | $25 | Annually |

**Penalty for late filing:** $250

### Registered Agent Requirement

All registered entities must have a California registered agent (individual or company) with a physical CA address.
